01 The Score

The crown slipped, and almost nobody at Netflix is panicking about money

For the first time, the most-watched video service on Earth is not Netflix. Across twenty international markets, measurement firm Digital i found that average daily viewing on YouTube climbed to 99.1 minutes per account in 2025 while Netflix slid to 93.4 — a reversal of the order that had held for a decade. The gap is only six minutes. The symbolism is the whole story.

Here is the twist that makes Netflix interesting rather than doomed: by the numbers that used to define it, the company is in excellent health. Full-year 2025 revenue came in around $45.2 billion, and management has guided 2026 to between $50.7 and $51.7 billion with operating margin expanding past 31%. Subscribers are still growing. Pricing still holds. This is not a financial crisis.

It is an attention crisis — and the distinction matters, because it quietly rewrote Netflix's most important metric. The number that keeps executives up at night is no longer subscribers. It is minutes. And the opponent stealing those minutes isn't Disney or Max or any other prestige library. It is the infinite scroll: YouTube and TikTok, the default feeds that have made attention a strictly zero-sum game.

02 The Stakes

Why minutes became the moat

The average U.S. household now pays for roughly 4.5 streaming services. In that crowd, the rule is brutal and simple: the app you forget to open is the app you cancel. Engagement is the single best leading indicator of churn, and churn is the tax that determines whether pricing power and ad inventory grow or rot.

Attention is upstream of everything. Minutes buy retention; retention buys pricing power; pricing power and engaged eyeballs buy a premium ad business. Lose the minutes and the rest erodes from the top.

Read that way, every major Netflix move since 2024 stops looking like a content strategy and starts looking like an attention strategy. Live events, games, the new vertical feed, the ad tier — none of them are really about what's on screen. They are all about how often you press play, and how long you stay before you bounce to a feed.

03 What's Working · Live

The 52-week habit loop

The clearest win in modern streaming is a wrestling show. Netflix's $5 billion, ten-year deal for WWE Raw delivered 525 million hours of WWE viewing in its first year, with Raw alone pulling 340 million and landing in the global Top 10 for 47 of 52 weeks. But the raw tonnage isn't the insight. The insight is in the cancellation data.

Ampere Analysis tracked who stayed. Among U.S. viewers who joined for Raw, only 18.2% churned within 60 days. Compare that to the one-off spectacles: the Jake Paul–Mike Tyson fight churned at 25.6%, and the NFL's Christmas Day games at 27.2%. The lesson is almost philosophical. A megafight is a fireworks show — spectacular, and then over. Raw is a standing weekly appointment. Spectacle spikes; habit retains.

The financial translation is direct. Analysts at TheWrap estimate WWE alone now saves Netflix roughly 1.25 million subscribers from churning every quarter, worldwide — value created before a single ad dollar is counted. As Ted Sarandos put it when the deal was signed, WWE is "52 weeks of live programming every year." It is a year-round retention anchor disguised as a sport.

The ad engine that live unlocked

Live viewers don't fast-forward. That single behavioral fact makes live the most valuable ad inventory Netflix has, and it is powering a genuine second business. Ad revenue roughly doubled to $1.5 billion in 2025 and Netflix expects it to double again to about $3 billion in 2026. The advertiser base has crossed 4,000 (up 70% year over year), the ad-supported plan now drives over 60% of new sign-ups where it's offered, and the research firm Omdia projects the ad business reaching $8 billion by 2030.

One discipline defines the whole strategy: Sarandos has been explicit that Netflix will not chase full-season league rights. It targets only the marquee, monetizable events — and even those it has learned to execute. After the embarrassing pixelation of the Tyson–Paul stream in 2024, the NFL Christmas Day broadcasts ran flawlessly, and Netflix has since locked a five-game NFL package for 2026. The doctrine is curated, not comprehensive.

04 What's Half-Working · Games

Gaming's slow burn

Games are the other half of the attention thesis, and they are nowhere near as far along. Bundled free with every subscription, they generate zero direct revenue by design — the entire point is keeping subscribers active inside the ecosystem. Co-CEO Greg Peters concedes Netflix is still "in the early stages of this rollout" with "a lot of work to go."

The strategy pivoted hard. Mobile downloads asked too much — storage, friction, intent — so in late 2025 Netflix moved to TV-based cloud streaming, with your phone as the controller and nothing to install. Around a third of members upgraded their TV clients. But actual play remains thin: only about 10% of eligible TV-enabled members have touched the games.

Telling, too, is which games work. The prestige titles aimed at core gamers — Monument Valley 3, Civilization VII — generate headlines. The engagement comes from the opposite end: living-room party games like Boggle, Pictionary, and LEGO Party. The win, when it comes, is social and casual, not hardcore. The big swing is a redesigned, cloud-based FIFA football title due late 2026 — the bet that a frictionless blockbuster on the biggest screen finally drives mass adoption. The thesis is sound. The scale isn't there yet.

05 The Playbook

Building the operating system of the living room

Under new Chief Product and Technology Officer Elizabeth Stone, the through-line of Netflix's next moves is one idea: stop being an app you visit and become the environment you live in. Four plays carry it.

1 — Kill choice paralysis

The infinite scroll of the home grid has become a tax on attention. Netflix is testing conversational, mood-based search powered by generative AI — describe a feeling or a situation, get a title — to shorten the distance between opening the app and pressing play. And to fight TikTok on its own turf, it has launched a mobile-first vertical "Clips" feed of snackable previews you swipe, play, save, or share.

2 — Make the screen shoppable

As the ad tier scales, Netflix is turning passive impressions into closed-loop commerce: shoppable pause ads and interactive mid-rolls (built with ad-tech partners like Kerv.ai) that let viewers buy via QR code or remote. It's blending content and commerce directly into its IP — think custom TurboTax campaigns woven into WWE broadcasts.

3 — Make live two-way

Building on weekly live's success, Netflix is adding real-time participation — "Moments" for bookmarking and sharing live clips, and live voting that lets sports and reality audiences shape a broadcast as it happens. Lean-back becomes lean-in.

4 — Spill into the real world

Physical "Netflix House" complexes extend the franchises into community fandom that loops attention back into the app. The digital and the physical reinforce each other.

Unify video, live, cloud gaming, and shoppable commerce under one AI-tuned interface, and you have a moat designed for a single purpose: keep the user inside, so they never bounce to a competing feed.

06 The Verdict

Is it working? Read the scorecard.

Strip away the strategy decks and the honest answer is split. One bet has decisively paid off, one is compounding, and two are still live rounds. First, the context that keeps it all in proportion: losing the attention crown did not mean losing the revenue crown — though it exposed how far ahead YouTube's machine now is, and that Disney's diversified empire is still larger than both.

Open bet

That last row is the real risk. Netflix's structural edge has always been the opposite of the scroll: appointment, prestige, the lean-back evening. By chasing vertical clips and frictionless feeds, it's choosing to fight YouTube and TikTok where they have a decade's head start and Netflix has no natural advantage. The danger isn't losing that fight outright — it's diluting the one thing it does better than anyone while trying.

The 93-minute problem was never that Netflix is losing. It's still one of the two giants of global attention. The real question is whether the future belongs to the lean-back appointment or the lean-in scroll — and Netflix, expensively, is hedging both.

So: is it working? On live, unambiguously, yes — that bet alone may justify the rest. On everything else, the meter is still running.
